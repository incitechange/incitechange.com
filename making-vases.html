Paint Skins · Glass Vessels

The skin remembers.

A pour happens once. The skin carries it somewhere else.

Photo coming

Paint skins applied to three glass vessels · acrylic on silicone backing, peeled and transferred

What a paint skin is

The pour preserved.

A paint skin is what happens when you pour acrylic paint onto a flat, non-stick surface — a silicone sheet on a board — let it dry completely, and peel it up. The dried film preserves everything that happened during the pour: the directional flow, the cell structure, the color separations, the moment the silicone chemistry opened a pocket between two colors. All of it is locked into the skin when the paint cures.

The skin is then transferred to a new surface. In this case, glass vases — curved, three-dimensional forms that the original pour knew nothing about. The skin wraps the curve, mod podge holds the edge, and a coat of clear acrylic seals and deepens the color. The inside of the glass stays pristine. Still functional. Still holds water.

The technique

Peel, wrap, seal.

The skins for these vases were poured using a flip cup on a silicone sheet — the same flip cup mechanics as a canvas pour, but the destination is temporary. The silicone backing releases cleanly when the paint is fully cured. The skin peels up in one piece, slightly tacky on the clean side, which helps it grip the glass before the adhesive sets.

Wrapping a curved form is a different problem than laying a skin flat. The material has to ease around the waist, conform to the shoulder, and meet itself at the seam without buckling. The clear coat that follows does two things: seals the edges permanently and makes the colors read about thirty percent richer than the dry skin alone.

The three vases

Same color family. Different origins.

The living room vase — the tallest of the three, with a flared top and a slight waist. Originally held cut flowers, now holds water bamboo. The skin on this one wasn’t made for a vase. It’s the paint that fell off the edge of a different painting entirely — runoff that dropped about a foot onto the plastic mat below and pooled exactly where gravity put it. No tilting, no intervention. The original painting is gone. The paint that escaped it isn’t.

The candle jar — shorter, cylindrical, originally a candle vessel, now holds an artificial plant. Same origin story as the first vase, different pour. A different painting’s runoff, a different day, a different pool of escaped paint that dried on the mat and turned out to be worth keeping. The cobalt is heavier here, the marbling tighter, the metallic shimmer more pronounced.

The office vase — the third form, hourglass-shaped, currently holding rooting wandering jew cuttings on the office windowsill. This skin was poured intentionally and thicker than the others. The thicker paint base is what produced the large, distinct cells visible in the lower half — more silicone, more paint weight, more separation between colors. Same color family, completely different surface character. This one looks like it came from a different body of work. It came from a different pour, with different variables, and the skin remembered all of them.

The inside

Still functional.

The clear coat seals the exterior. The interior is untouched glass — smooth, waterproof, unchanged. All three vases hold water. All three have held plants or flowers at some point. One held a candle before it held anything else.

The technique makes functional objects. That matters. A skin on a vase isn’t decoration applied to something useful — it’s a record of a physical event wrapped around something that keeps doing its job.
