Resin and Mica · Cup Turner

The design tool is rotation.

Not gravity. Not air. Centrifugal force.

Photo coming

Resin and mica powder on thermal cup · cup turner technique · silver, purple, blue, teal, olive

The object

A promotional cup, repurposed.

The cup started as a promotional item from a team building event — a thermal cup with a lid, the kind that sits in a cabinet for years. It didn’t stay in the cabinet.

It now lives on the side table in the living room, holding pens and Pax’s grooming brush. The cup is velcroed to the table. This is because Pax has learned that rubbing against the brush handle is an effective way to request a spa treatment, and without the velcro, his enthusiasm would send the whole thing to the floor.

The technique

Centrifugal force as the design tool.

Resin and mica powder work differently from acrylic paint. Resin is a two-part liquid that cures hard and glossy. Mica is a mineral pigment that catches and reflects light — it shifts depending on the angle, which is why the surface reads differently every time you look at it.

A cup turner spins the substrate continuously on a horizontal axis while the resin is applied. As it rotates, the material is pulled outward by centrifugal force and distributed across the surface. The horizontal banding forms as the cup spins — each color settling into its own orbit before the next is applied. The layering sequence determines which colors end up where.

This was the first time working with resin and mica. The color selection was intentional: silver, purple, blue, teal, olive — a darker and more complex palette than the blue and green work that came before it.

Different physics

No gravity. No cells.

Every other piece in this body of work uses gravity as the primary force. Paint pours rely on weight, tilt, and flow. The cup turner removes gravity from the equation and replaces it with rotation.

The result looks completely different. No cells, no directional sweeps, no pooling at the edges. Instead: precise horizontal bands with soft transitions between colors, mica shimmer running consistently around the full circumference, a surface that reads as geological — like a cross-section of something that formed over a very long time rather than something made in an afternoon.

It didn’t form over a long time. But the resin cure is slower than acrylic. You apply, you walk away, you come back to what it decided to do while you weren’t watching.
