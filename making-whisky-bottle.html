Vertical Pour · Applied Skin · Found Stopper

The bottle depicts what it does.

A portrait of falling smoke, made from falling paint.

Photo coming

Vertical pour on found whisky bottle · green base, black and silver skin · packing foam stopper

The concept

Smoke that falls.

The incense I burn produces smoke that falls rather than rises. It’s heavier than air — it cascades downward from the source, which is the opposite of what most people expect smoke to do. That behavior is the subject of this piece.

The bottle needed to show it.

The green pour

Paint falling the way smoke falls.

The bottle stood upright during the pour. Green paint applied to the neck and shoulders, gravity pulling it straight down the body of the glass — no tilting, no intervention. The paint fell the way the smoke falls. The streaks and runs and pooling at the base are a record of that descent, not an accident to be corrected.

That’s the ground layer. The bottle as landscape. The thing the smoke moves through.

The skin

The smoke itself.

The smoke itself is a paint skin — black and silver, poured flat on a silicone sheet, cured, peeled, and wrapped around the bottle over the green. The cellular and swirling structure that fluid dynamics produces when two resistant materials meet — the deep nested rings, the flowing channels, the dense opacity of the black against the metallic silver — reads exactly like smoke caught in a photograph. Not illustrated. Recorded.

The skin technique is the same one used for the vases. The palette is completely different. Black and silver against green glass reads as weight and density rather than light and transparency. This is the other end of what the medium can do.

The stopper

What’s inside.

The stopper is made from white bendable packing foam — the kind that compresses and conforms. Curled into a shape that fits the bottle neck and seals it airtight. The incense cones are stored inside until they’re needed.
