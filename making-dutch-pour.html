Dutch Pour · Cigar Box Lid

Darkness emerging from light.

White ground. Blues and greens on top. Air does the rest.

The setup

An inversion.

Most of the pours in this body of work use a dark ground — color emerging from darkness. This one inverts that. The base is white fluid paint, and the blues and greens were laid on top of it as stripes. Silicone oil added to multiple colors to encourage cell development. Paint mixed thin. White acrylic is among the heaviest pigments. It sinks. That physical fact is what makes the composition possible.

The technique

Air as the tool.

The blow dryer went across the paint pool, not with it — perpendicular to the stripe direction. That cross-movement opened the cells and pushed color away from the center, letting the white ground come through between them. The white stripe on top of the color stack pushes everything else up and outward when it sinks, spreading the blues and greens in a way that thinner paint alone wouldn’t achieve.

The substrate

Small and dense.

The cigar box lid is small and rigid with hard edges. Paint doesn’t wrap around — it hits the boundary and stops. The scale of the cells relative to the small surface makes the composition feel dense and compressed. Nothing has room to thin out or drift. The close-up reads like a reef section under glass — deep cobalt forms, iridescent green-gold cell walls, dark tessellated structures that look like something that grew rather than something that was made.

Technical notes

PaintBlues, greens, black, white fluid acrylics — mixed thin
Silicone oilAdded to multiple colors
TechniqueDutch pour — stripes layered, air stream perpendicular
ToolBlow dryer, cross-directional pass
SupportCigar box lid
GroundWhite
StateFinished
