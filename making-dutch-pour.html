Dutch Pour · Cigar Box Lid

Darkness emerging from light.

White ground. Blues and greens on top. Air does the rest.

Photo coming

Dutch pour on cigar box lid · blues, greens, black on white ground · blow dryer, perpendicular pass

The setup

An inversion.

Most of the pours in this body of work use a dark ground — color emerging from darkness. This one inverts that. The base is white fluid paint, and the blues and greens were laid on top of it as stripes, one color on top of the next. Silicone oil added to multiple colors to encourage cell development. Paint mixed thin.

White acrylic is among the heaviest pigments. It sinks. That physical fact is what makes the composition possible.

The technique

Air as the tool.

A Dutch pour doesn’t use gravity and tilt the way a flip cup or swipe pour does. The tool is air — a directed stream that pushes the pooled paint across the surface. Where the air hits, the paint moves. Where it doesn’t, it stays.

The blow dryer went across the paint pool, not with it — perpendicular to the stripe direction. That cross-movement is what opened the cells and pushed color away from the center, letting the white ground come through between them. The air separates incompatible fluids at speed, which produces sharper cell walls than you get from tilt work alone.

What white does when it sinks

The heaviest color on top.

The white stripe on top of the color stack seems counterintuitive. If white is the ground, why add more of it on top?

Because when the heaviest pigment sinks through the colors beneath it, it pushes everything else up and outward. The white doesn’t stay on top — it moves down and out, carrying the blues and greens with it, spreading them across the surface in a way that thinner paint alone wouldn’t achieve. The result is a white field with color riding on and through it, rather than color sitting on a white background.

The substrate

Small and dense.

The cigar box lid is small and rigid with hard edges. Paint doesn’t wrap around — it hits the boundary and stops. The scale of the cells relative to the small surface makes the composition feel dense and compressed. Nothing has room to thin out or drift. Every square inch is doing something.

The close-up reads like a reef section under glass — deep cobalt forms, iridescent green-gold cell walls, dark tessellated structures that look like something that grew rather than something that was made.
