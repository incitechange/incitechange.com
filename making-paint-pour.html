The medium

Art that works like a system.

Paint pouring is not random. It looks unpredictable because the forces involved are subtle, but they are real and they respond to decisions. The weight of the pigment relative to the pouring medium, the chemical additives that change surface tension, the sequence and angle of the pour, the temperature of the paint — all of these affect what gravity does when the fluid hits the canvas.

What draws me to it is the same thing that draws me to any complex system: there are variables, the variables interact, and if you understand the interactions well enough you can set up conditions that produce outcomes you want — while still leaving room for the system to do things you didn't expect.

The variables

What actually changes what happens.

Pigment density

Heavier pigments sink. Lighter ones rise. The relative density of each color in the pour determines the layering — which colors appear on top, which sink through others.

Chemical additives

Silicone oil and other additives change the surface tension within and between colors. This is what creates cells — the distinctive circular forms that appear as incompatible fluids try to separate.

Pour sequence

The order colors hit the canvas matters. What's poured first gets pushed by what comes after. The sequence affects the final composition even when the pour is technically identical.

Tilt and movement

After pouring, the canvas moves. The angle and direction of the tilt, how many times and how quickly — these spread the fluid and create the final composition. This is where gravity becomes intentional.

The palette

Blues, teals, and greens — with the occasional surprise.

The dominant color story across the body of work is the same palette that runs through everything else: blues, teals, and blue-greens. Sometimes with a poppy yellow-green that appears whether you planned for it or not.

The Golfhole Series is the current active project — wall-scale pours that use the physics of the medium more deliberately, designing the pour sequence around what gravity will do with each color combination.

The analytical appeal of paint pouring is real — but so is the humility it requires. You can set up the conditions carefully and still have the pour go somewhere unexpected. The variables you can't fully control include the temperature in the room, minor inconsistencies in how well the paint was mixed, and the precise moment you decide to stop tilting. That's not a failure of the system. It's the point.
