Swipe Pour · Arteza Metallics

Across the room.

I had a shower curtain I loved. I decided to make the painting that went with it.

Photo coming

Swipe pour on vertical canvas · Arteza metallics, Azteca Ultramarine Blue base · installed in bathroom

The decision

Made to match.

Most paintings start with a surface and an intention. This one started with a shower curtain — midcentury modern geometric shapes, a specific color story of teal and cobalt and green that I kept coming back to. I didn’t want to copy it. I wanted to make something that lived in the same visual space. Something that would read as a response from across the room.

The color match was deliberate. Azteca Ultramarine Blue as the base — the same non-metallic ultramarine from the Bioluminescent pour, same formula of water and Liquitex pouring medium, but less water here. Thicker paint produces larger cells, and I wanted structure in this one.

The swipe

One pass per zone.

A swipe pour works differently from a Dutch pour. Instead of using air to move the paint, you use a physical tool — something that drags across the wet surface. The tool matters.

I pooled lines of color directly on top of the wet ultramarine base — Arteza metallics, not mixed, sitting almost touching. Then swiped each zone once with a three-to-four-inch width of wet paper towel. Once only, to preserve the structure of each line.

The wet paper towel is not the typical tool. Unlike a palette knife or card, it absorbs as it moves — it picks up ultramarine from the base as it travels across and carries it into the path. That’s what gives the leading edge its softness. The cells in a swipe are also larger than in a Dutch pour because the paint sits thicker. The motion opens them differently — a long, directional opening rather than the radial burst you get from air.

The geometry

Vertical organization.

The shower curtain has vertical organization — shapes arranged in zones, each with its own color logic, reading as a whole from a distance. I tried to work with that same underlying structure. The pooled lines ran vertically. The swipe moved with them, not across them. Each zone has its own cellular character because each pass was independent.

The Ultramarine shows at the top and bottom of the canvas — the base color exposed where the swipe passed, giving texture and depth rather than a flat border. It frames the piece the same way the curtain’s border frames its pattern. The canvas is hung vertically to match the curtain’s proportions.

Where it lives

Across the room.

The painting hangs in the bathroom. The curtain is still there. They don’t match exactly — the physics of a pour and the geometry of printed fabric produce different things. But they’re in conversation. They read the same from across the room, which was the goal from the start.

There’s something satisfying about commissioning art from yourself. You know exactly what you want. The deadline is flexible. The client is easy to please.

Technical notes

PaintArteza Metallic Acrylics, Azteca Ultramarine Blue (non-metallic base)
MediumWater + Liquitex Pouring Medium (less water than Bioluminescent — thicker consistency)
ToolWet paper towel, 3–4 inch width, single pass per zone
TechniqueSwipe pour — pooled lines on wet base, one directional pass each
SupportVertical canvas, gallery wrap
StateFinished, installed
Lives withThe shower curtain it was made to match
