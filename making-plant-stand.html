Structural Cardboard · Paper Mache · Fluid Pour

It looks like cast iron. It is cardboard.

The first piece in a series about what cardboard furniture can actually be.

The premise

Cardboard as a real material.

Cardboard is not a temporary material. Corrugated cardboard properly oriented and laminated is strong in compression — strong enough to function as outdoor furniture. This piece sits outside the front door holding a plant. It has done this through Portland weather. It is still there.

The build

Wheat paste and paper mache.

The form is three-legged — a round top on three rectangular legs. Each component is four to five layers of corrugated cardboard, cut to shape and laminated together with wheat paste. The wheat paste is the traditional bookbinding adhesive — flour and water cooked to a thick slurry. It bonds the layers flat and hard as it dries.

The laminated cardboard is strong but rough. Paper mache solved that — several coats applied over the legs and top, each allowed to dry fully before the next. The paper mache fills the texture, rounds the edges slightly, and creates a unified surface that takes paint the way wood does.

The surface

Volcanic.

The legs are painted with Azteca silver metallic. The top is a fluid pour — black and white with silver moving through it, poured directly onto the paper mache surface. The swirling forms and deep voids look volcanic. Molten stone cooling, or the surface of something that formed under pressure. The drips over the edge where the paint ran during the pour are still there. They record the moment correctly.

What this is the beginning of

First in a series.

This is the first piece. The series premise is simple: cardboard is available, cheap, and structurally underestimated. Wheat paste and paper mache are labor-intensive but cost almost nothing. The question is how far the approach scales — larger forms, more complex geometry, different surface treatments. The plant stand is a proof of concept. It works. The series continues.
