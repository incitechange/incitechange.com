Feb – Jul 2025 · Contract

When the process
is the problem.

How to stabilize a complex web delivery program without blowing up what's already working.

Process Design Web Delivery Program Management MS Project SharePoint Power BI QA Workflows
01
The situation

A program that was running — just not cleanly.

I joined a web delivery program managed through a consulting firm, serving a large enterprise technology client. Multiple vendor teams — web, design, content, and development — were collaborating across a segmented Microsoft Teams environment with carefully controlled channel access and confidentiality requirements.

The program was moving. Pages were being built. But the underlying delivery infrastructure was fragile in ways that weren't visible until you looked closely: schedules duplicated across multiple Excel files, image assets managed through screenshots and workarounds, copy handed off through color-coded Word documents with no version control, and a shared observation matrix that would have collapsed under the load of a real QA push.

Nobody had mapped the full picture yet. That became my first job.

02
My role

Web Delivery Program Manager.

I owned the master project schedule, managed cross-vendor handoffs, ran partner-facing milestone reviews, and served as the connective tissue between teams who couldn't always see each other's work.

I also owned something less formal but equally important: the relationship between the web team and a design vendor that had started the engagement with significant tension on both sides. One of my first moves was to set up a standing weekly meeting specifically to rebuild that working relationship. By the time I left, that vendor wrote one of the most generous professional references I have received.

03
What I found

Three workflows. Same underlying problem.

After mapping the current state across all three major workflows, the structural picture was consistent. None of this was negligence — it was a set of reasonable workarounds that had accumulated over time into a system with no slack left in it.

Schedule management

Master schedule maintained in MS Project internally, then manually translated into separate Excel workback documents for each partner team. Three versions of the truth, no automatic sync, and growing risk that partners were planning against outdated timelines.

Copy handoffs

Manual compare-and-merge between two Teams environments, tracked through color-coded Word document highlights. Yellow meant changed copy. Red meant delete. Red font meant placeholder. It worked until someone missed a post, went out of office, or opened the wrong version.

Image and asset management

Assets from one team couldn't be accessed directly by another due to permissions gaps. Workaround: the PM screenshots the image, enters it manually into an Asset Matrix spreadsheet, and hopes the metadata holds. Fragile by design.

QA launch risk

Twenty people were about to share a single QA tracking spreadsheet at launch — no row locking, no audit history, no recovery path if it broke. This was the most immediate structural risk in the program.

04
What I built

Stabilize now. Modernize next.

I didn't propose a full rebuild. The program had commitments and timelines that didn't have room for that. Instead I built a two-horizon plan.

Short term

QA intake workflow

Designed and piloted a QA intake workflow using Microsoft Forms feeding into a SharePoint-backed tracker. Replaced the shared Excel QA sheet with structured intake — every submission gets a timestamp, an owner, and a record that can't be accidentally overwritten. Documented the rollout path to full implementation.

Medium term

Milestone visibility model

Drafted a milestone visibility model using Teams, SharePoint, and Power BI — designed to give executive stakeholders a live readiness view without exposing the confidential internal schedule. Role-based access meant each audience saw exactly what they needed, nothing more.

I also documented the full current-state picture across all three workflow areas in plain language: what the process was, what the risks were, and what a pragmatic improvement path looked like. Not a complaint document. A map with a route marked on it.

05
The honest part

What I left behind.

The engagement ended before the remediation plan was fully implemented. That was consistent with the contract risk I had flagged from week one — the timeline was ambitious and the program had structural vulnerabilities that needed time to address.

What I left behind was a clear diagnosis, a tested proof-of-concept, a documented path forward, and a set of partner relationships in better shape than I found them.

This is some of the most honest PM work in my portfolio — not a triumphant launch story, but a real account of what it looks like to walk into a fragile system, map it carefully, and hand the next person a better starting position than you had.
